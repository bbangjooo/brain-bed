Features

Three things happen. None require your judgment.

It Watches

You open Claude Code at 9 AM and Cursor at 9:05. By 10:30 you've added Gemini CLI in a third terminal. Brain Bed sees all of them. It tracks session duration, prompt frequency, tool-switching speed, and rest gaps. A number appears in your menu bar: your Brain Fry Index, 0 to 100.

It Catches

BFI hits 70. You've been bouncing between three AI tools for two hours straight without a single pause longer than 45 seconds. You just re-read the same Claude output for the third time and retained nothing. That's not reviewing — that's a screensaver with anxiety. Brain Bed flags the pattern.

It Resets

BFI crosses the threshold. The screen goes full. The keyboard stops responding. Classical music plays. A timer counts down from five minutes. You're annoyed for about thirty seconds and then something loosens behind your eyes. When the timer ends, you look at the code. You see the bug immediately. It was on line 47 the whole time.

AI Brain Fry

They called it "crack for nerds." They weren't joking.

When we posted about AI coding fatigue on Reddit, it got 47K views and 50+ comments. Developers called it "crack for nerds." An ex-addict in the thread said "the dopamine hits are real — this is basically the same thing." The auto-generated TL;DR said: "The consensus is a resounding YES, Claude Code Brain Fry is a very real thing."

AI coding tools are the most productive thing that ever happened to developers — and the most compulsive. One more prompt. One more agent run. One more hit. Your brain doesn't distinguish between "productive work" and "compulsive repetition" when the reward schedule is this fast.

⚡ Prompt fatigue
You keep rephrasing the same question, unable to articulate what you actually need.
🧊 Decision paralysis
You accept AI suggestions without reviewing them, or reject everything on instinct.
🌀 Context blur
You forget which project you're working on or what you were trying to accomplish 10 minutes ago.
🔥 Compulsive prompting
You keep starting new AI sessions even though you know you should stop.

AI brain fry isn't a character flaw — it's what happens when machines remove every speed limit and your brain doesn't get an upgrade. BCG found that brain fry increases major mistakes by 39%. The good news: it's reversible with breaks. The bad news: you won't take them yourself. Brain Bed forces the break you'll never choose to take.

You can't ctrl+Z cognitive overload.

Five minutes. That's the cost. The return is the bug you catch, the design flaw you spot, the clarity hiding behind three hours of prompts.
