## Problem Section

### Header
You shipped more code this quarter than ever. You also feel worse than ever.

### Lead
AI didn't just make coding faster. It removed every natural pause that used to protect you. No compiling. No waiting. No context switch. Just you and three AI tools open at once, bouncing between them like tabs in your brain. Reddit called it "crack for nerds." They were describing a real phenomenon.

### Pain Point 1: The Context Switch That Never Ends

**You're not coding anymore. You're herding robots.** Claude is refactoring module A. Cursor is autocompleting module B. You just asked Gemini about module C. You're holding all three contexts in your head, switching between terminals every 30 seconds, reviewing output you barely read. You're not in flow -- you're in a blender. The old context switch had a cooldown: you'd walk to the whiteboard, wait for a build, grab coffee. AI tools respond in seconds. Your brain never gets the gap.

> "Six worktrees open, four half-written features, two 'quick fixes' that spawned rabbit holes, and a growing sense that I'm losing the plot entirely."

Without Brain Bed, you scatter until you crash. With Brain Bed, a score catches the spiral before you merge something stupid.

### Pain Point 2: "One More Prompt" Is the New "One More Hit"

**The feedback loop is the problem.** One prompt, instant result, dopamine. Another prompt, better result, more dopamine. Your brain doesn't distinguish between "productive work" and "compulsive repetition" when the reward schedule is this fast. Developers on Reddit called AI coding "crack for nerds." An ex-addict in the thread said "the dopamine hits are real -- this is basically the same thing." You keep going not because the work demands it, but because stopping feels worse than continuing.

> "I had to actively force myself to leave home because I was getting consumed by it on the weekend."

Without Brain Bed, the loop has no exit. With Brain Bed, the keyboard locks and the loop breaks.

### Pain Point 3: You Think You're Productive. The Data Says Otherwise.

**The cruelest part is the confidence.** METR ran a controlled study: developers believed AI tools made them 20% faster. Actual measured performance: 19% slower. That's a 40-percentage-point gap between how productive you feel and how productive you are. You're switching between tools, approving output, shipping PRs -- and it feels fast. But your judgment degrades with every context switch, and BCG found that developers experiencing brain fry make 39% more major mistakes.

> "I caught myself rereading the same stuff, second-guessing way more than usual."

Without Brain Bed, you trust a brain that's lying to you. With Brain Bed, a number tells you the truth.

## Solution Section

### Header
Three things happen. None of them require your judgment.

### Lead
By the time you need Brain Bed, you're too fried to decide to use it. That's the design.

### Step 1: It Watches

You open Claude Code at 9 AM and Cursor at 9:05. By 10:30 you've added Gemini CLI in a third terminal. Brain Bed sees all of them. It tracks session duration, prompt frequency, tool-switching speed, and rest gaps. A number appears in your menu bar: your Brain Fry Index, 0 to 100. At 23, you don't notice it. At 41, you glance and keep going. At 58, you think "I should probably take a break" and open another terminal instead. Brain Bed expected that.

### Step 2: It Catches

BFI hits 70. You've been bouncing between three AI tools for two hours straight without a single pause longer than 45 seconds. You just re-read the same Claude output for the third time and retained nothing. That's not reviewing -- that's a screensaver with anxiety. Brain Bed flags the pattern. A notification appears. You dismiss it. Brain Bed noted that too.

### Step 3: It Resets

BFI crosses the threshold. The screen goes full. The keyboard stops responding. Classical music plays. A timer counts down from five minutes. You sit there. You breathe. You're annoyed for about thirty seconds and then something loosens behind your eyes. When the timer ends, you get your keyboard back. You look at the code. You see the bug immediately. It was on line 47 the whole time.

## Feature Highlights

**Brain Fry Index**
A real-time cognitive load score from 0 to 100. Calculated from your actual AI tool usage patterns -- session length, prompt frequency, rest gaps. Not a guess. A measurement.

**Keyboard Lock**
Uses macOS Accessibility APIs to disable keyboard input during forced breaks. Not a modal you can dismiss. Not a notification you can swipe. Your keyboard stops working until the timer ends.

**Classical Music**
Curated tracks play during resets. Silence makes you reach for your phone. Music gives your brain something that isn't the next prompt.

**System Tray**
Lives in your menu bar. Shows current BFI at a glance. No window to manage. No app to arrange. One number, always visible.

**Privacy First**
Everything runs locally. No cloud. No accounts. No telemetry. Your coding patterns never leave your machine. Works offline. Works air-gapped.

**Open Source**
MIT licensed. Read every line. Fork it. Fix it. We don't want your data. We want you to rest.

## FAQ Section

### Q: Does it actually lock my keyboard?
**A:** Yes. macOS Accessibility APIs, not a polite suggestion. You grant the permission once. After that, when your BFI crosses the threshold, your keyboard is disabled until the timer ends. Your mouse still works. You can watch the countdown. You just can't type.

### Q: Isn't forcing a break kind of extreme?
**A:** Cold Turkey proved that strictness is a feature, not a bug. Every break timer that relies on you choosing to stop will fail -- because the part of your brain that makes that choice is the part that's fried. Using a tool to manage this is the smart move, not the weak one.

### Q: I don't think I have a problem. I just like coding.
**A:** Most developers don't think they have a problem. The METR study found a 40-percentage-point gap between how fast developers think they are and how fast they actually are. Brain Bed isn't here to diagnose you. It's here to show you a number and let you decide what it means.

### Q: What if I'm in the middle of something important?
**A:** You were in the middle of something important three hours ago. Right now you're in the middle of diminishing returns. The break is five minutes. Your work will still be there. It will go better after.

### Q: "Crack for nerds" -- isn't that a bit much?
**A:** We didn't come up with it. Developers did. When we posted about AI coding fatigue on Reddit, it got 47K views and 50+ comments. "Crack for nerds" was their phrase. An ex-addict in the thread said "the dopamine hits are real -- this is basically the same thing." We're not diagnosing anyone. We built the tool that interrupts the loop.

### Q: Which AI tools does it monitor?
**A:** Claude Code, Gemini CLI, Codex CLI, and Cursor. Brain Bed detects active CLI processes and terminal sessions. If you use a tool we don't support yet, open an issue on GitHub.

### Q: Is it really free?
**A:** Free. Open source. MIT licensed. No freemium. No premium tier. No "upgrade for longer breaks." The entire source code is on GitHub. It will stay free.

### Q: Does it collect any data?
**A:** No. Everything runs locally on your Mac. No accounts, no cloud sync, no telemetry, no analytics. Brain Bed doesn't phone home. Ever.

## Tone Notes

- No exclamation marks anywhere. Confidence is quiet.
- No "meditation" in any front-facing copy. The app forces breaks. What happens during the break (classical music, breathing) is described in feature details, never sold in headlines.
- Never say "you're addicted." Use community quotes ("developers call it..."), external attribution ("AI removed the governor, not you"), or tool pride ("using a tool for this is the smart move").
- "Crack for nerds" comes from the community (Reddit, 47K views). Always attribute it -- "developers called it," never "we call it."
- Context-switching chaos is the default scene, not late-night exhaustion. The developer has 3 AI tools open, 6 worktrees, bouncing between terminals. This is more universal and more urgent than someone tired at 2 AM.
- Every section implies a before/after. Without Brain Bed, the bad thing. With Brain Bed, the good thing. One beat.
- Positive outcome framing: not "stop burning out" but "catch the bug you'd have missed."
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Scannable by a developer scrolling at 2 AM.
- Humor is dry and structural, never cute. Think README tone -- the kind of writing where you can't tell if it's a joke or documentation.
- Technical specificity over marketing abstraction. "macOS Accessibility APIs" is more trustworthy than "advanced technology."
- The product is opinionated and that's the feature. Brain Bed doesn't ask. It acts.
